### unit 1
It completely depends on how much economic benefit there is, weighed against environmental damage: the best way to determine this is an auction. Oil extraction does not directly provide jobs per se, as increasing oil production necessarily diverts resources from a different sector of the economy, as dictated by the production possibilities frontier. However, if the US has a comparative advantage, by exploiting her natural resources, the gain is greater than the opportunity cost, leading to materially more goods, even if the oil is not directly used, as it can be traded in the global market for goods. Assuming perfectly efficient trade, increasing oil extraction should not significantly lower the oil price, though as one of the largest producers, a large site does have some impact on a resource with a somewhat low price elasticity of supply. In aggregate, the decision is not black and white: it totally depends on the true net economic benefit: where you must subtract the opportunity cost and damage to the environment. The opportunity cost is already represented by labor costs and cost of capital: the amount you would have to pay a worker for them to work on oil instead, but environmental damage is not internalized. The best path is for the government to hold an auction over the rights to drill: if the price is deemed high enough, it is obviously positive for the economy, while the surplus can be used to fund environmental conservation, satisfying the activists. In fact, while I wrote this before searching up the current affairs, indeed this is close to what the current administration has done: they have decided to offer lease sales, with the priority of maximizing oil development. The best way forward would be to dedicate a portion of sales to environmental conservation, simultaneously reducing activist opposition.
